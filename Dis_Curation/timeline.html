32000 BC

In the times of the bible and ancient wars, in Ancient Babylonia were known to have dyed their fingernails to instill fear amongst their enemies.

3000 BC

The first nail treatments used as a beauty routine was born by women in Ancient China. They would soak their nails over night to absorb the colour of miztures made with beeswax, gelatin, and egg whites, for a natural tone, while for stronger colours they would use flowers and dyes.

1300 BC

Egypt, known for their impressive use of paints and pigments had queens, like Cleopatra and Nefertiti who would boast powerful red colours on their nail, as the stronger the red, the more power they would possess. They were known to use henna to dye their nails, however there are myths that they used blood.

600 BC

Further in Ancient Chinese heritage, the colour of ones finger nails became a staple of stature and social ranking. Those of the higher classes painted their nails red and black which sybolised strength and boldness. The general population were only permitted to wear pale and simple colours to maintain their modesty.

19th Century

The first commercial nail salons were opened in Paris offering services to both men and women,Especially in the Victorian Era, having clean and well manicured nails was a sign of good manners and clean health.

20th Century

The european style of manicurism made its way to United States, while still in France, Michelle Manard, a Makeup artist, worked on creating a type of nail lacquer that was similiar to the high gloss paint that was used on cars. With the introduction of high Technicolour movies and television, the longing for bright, powerful long lasting quality nail polish was in high request.

1950-Today

From the bright dazzling colours of the 1950's to the muted and mellow tones of the 60's back to the eclectic colours of the 70's modern styles nail polish have simply led to an era of anything and everything. All shapes, lenghts, colours, patterns, gems, having a work of art on your hands is a regular and popularies expectation, for everyone.
